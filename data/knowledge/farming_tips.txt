===== SOIL HEALTH & PREPARATION =====

Soil Testing
Test your soil every 2 years before planting. Collect soil from 5-6 spots in your field at 15cm depth.
Mix samples together and send to nearest Krishi Vigyan Kendra (KVK) for free testing.
Soil test tells you pH, nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium levels and what fertilizer you actually need.
Ideal soil pH for most crops: 6.0 to 7.0. If too acidic, apply lime. If too alkaline, apply gypsum.

Improving Soil Fertility Naturally
Add farmyard manure (FYM) at 10-15 tonnes per hectare before sowing. It improves soil structure and water retention.
Compost kitchen and crop waste for 45-60 days. Apply 5 tonnes per hectare.
Green manure crops like Dhaincha or Sunhemp: sow them and plough them back into soil before flowering.
Vermicompost is the best organic option. Apply 2-3 tonnes per hectare for excellent results.
Avoid burning crop stubble. It destroys beneficial soil microorganisms and reduces organic matter.

Crop Rotation
Never grow the same crop in the same field two seasons in a row.
Rotate cereals with legumes. Example: Rice followed by Chickpea, or Wheat followed by Moong.
Legumes fix nitrogen in soil naturally and reduce fertilizer cost for the next crop.
Good rotation for South India: Rice → Groundnut → Vegetables → Rice

===== IRRIGATION & WATER MANAGEMENT =====

Drip Irrigation
Drip irrigation saves 40-50% water compared to flood irrigation.
Best for vegetables, sugarcane, banana, and orchards.
Government subsidy available: up to 55% subsidy for small farmers under PM Krishi Sinchayee Yojana.
Install drip lines 30-45cm apart for vegetables. Water schedule: 1-2 hours daily in summer.

Sprinkler Irrigation
Good for wheat, groundnut, vegetables and uneven terrain.
Apply water early morning or evening to reduce evaporation loss.
Saves 30-40% water compared to flood irrigation.

Managing Waterlogging
Waterlogged fields damage roots and spread fungal diseases.
Make drainage channels at field borders 30cm deep.
Raised bed planting helps water drain away from roots.
If field stays wet for more than 2 days after rain, open drainage channels immediately.

Irrigation Schedule by Crop
Wheat: Irrigate at crown root initiation (21 days), tillering (45 days), flowering (65 days), grain filling (85 days).
Tomato: Water every 3-4 days in summer, every 6-7 days in winter. Stop 1 week before harvest.
Onion: Water every 7-10 days. Stop completely 15 days before harvest for good storage quality.
Rice: Maintain 5cm standing water during vegetative stage. Drain field 10 days before harvest.

===== FERTILIZER MANAGEMENT =====

NPK Basics
N = Nitrogen: promotes leafy green growth. Source: Urea, DAP.
P = Phosphorus: promotes root development and flowering. Source: DAP, SSP.
K = Potassium: improves crop quality and disease resistance. Source: MOP (Muriate of Potash).

Fertilizer Dose for Wheat (per hectare)
Nitrogen: 120 kg. Phosphorus: 60 kg. Potassium: 40 kg.
Apply half nitrogen + full phosphorus + full potassium as basal dose at sowing.
Apply remaining half nitrogen at first irrigation (crown root stage).

Fertilizer Dose for Rice (per hectare)
Nitrogen: 100 kg. Phosphorus: 50 kg. Potassium: 50 kg.
Apply full phosphorus and potassium as basal. Split nitrogen into 3 equal doses.

Fertilizer Dose for Tomato (per hectare)
Nitrogen: 150 kg. Phosphorus: 75 kg. Potassium: 75 kg.
Apply micronutrients especially Boron and Zinc for better fruit setting.

Avoiding Fertilizer Mistakes
Never apply urea on dry soil. Always irrigate before or after urea application.
Do not mix urea and DAP together. Apply separately.
Over-fertilizing with nitrogen causes excessive leafy growth and attracts pests.
Zinc deficiency is common in Indian soils. Apply zinc sulphate 25 kg per hectare if leaves show yellowing.

===== PEST MANAGEMENT =====

Integrated Pest Management (IPM)
IPM means using multiple methods to control pests with minimum chemicals.
Steps: Monitor crops twice a week. Identify pest correctly before spraying anything.
Use yellow sticky traps for whiteflies and thrips. Use pheromone traps for bollworm and fruit borer.
Only spray pesticide when pest count exceeds the economic threshold level.

Natural Pest Control Methods
Neem oil spray: 5ml per litre of water. Controls aphids, whiteflies, mites. Spray in evening.
Neem cake: Apply 250 kg per hectare in soil to control soil pests and nematodes.
Garlic-chilli spray: Blend 100g garlic + 50g chilli in 1 litre water. Dilute 1:10. Spray on leaves.
Encourage natural enemies: Do not spray when you see ladybird beetles, spiders, or wasps in field.

Common Pests and Control
Aphids: Small soft insects on undershoots. Spray imidacloprid 0.3ml per litre or neem oil.
Whiteflies: Tiny white flies on leaf undersides. Yellow sticky traps + thiamethoxam 0.2g per litre.
Thrips: Cause silvery streaks on leaves. Spinosad 0.3ml per litre spray is very effective.
Caterpillars and borers: Use Bt (Bacillus thuringiensis) spray 2g per litre. Safe and organic.

===== SEED & SOWING PRACTICES =====

Seed Treatment
Always treat seeds before sowing to protect from soil-borne diseases and insects.
Fungal treatment: Thiram or Carbendazim 2g per kg seed. Mix dry and sow immediately.
Bio-treatment: Trichoderma viride 4g per kg seed. Promotes root growth and disease resistance.
For legumes: Rhizobium culture 5g per kg seed. Helps fix nitrogen from air.

Seed Selection
Always buy certified seeds from government authorised dealers or cooperative societies.
Check seed packet for germination percentage (should be above 85%) and expiry date.
Store seeds in cool dry place in airtight containers. Never store near fertilizers or pesticides.

Sowing Tips
Sow at correct seed depth. Too deep = poor germination. Too shallow = dries out.
Wheat: 5-6cm depth. Tomato seedlings: transplant when 4-5 weeks old. Onion: 1-2cm depth.
Maintain correct spacing to allow air circulation and reduce disease spread.
Always sow after soil has enough moisture. Dry sowing followed by irrigation also works for wheat.

===== POST-HARVEST PRACTICES =====

Proper Harvesting
Harvest at right maturity. Harvesting too early reduces quality and weight. Too late causes losses.
Wheat: Harvest when grains are hard and golden yellow. Moisture content should be below 14%.
Tomato: Harvest at breaker stage (just turning red) for distant markets. Full red for local sale.
Onion: Harvest when 50-60% tops have fallen over naturally.

Storage Tips
Dry harvested produce to safe moisture levels before storage.
Clean storage area thoroughly. Remove old crop residues. Apply malathion spray to walls and floor.
Use hermetic bags for grain storage. They prevent insect damage without chemicals.
Onion storage: spread in single layer in well-ventilated shed. Never pile more than 30cm height.
Never store wet or damaged produce with good produce. One rotten item can spoil the entire lot.

Reducing Post-Harvest Losses
Post-harvest losses in India average 15-30% for vegetables and fruits.
Key causes: rough handling, lack of shade, no grading, poor transport.
Simple solutions: use crates not sacks for vegetables, transport at night or early morning, sort and grade before selling.
